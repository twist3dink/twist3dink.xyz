The CIA Triad: Not That CIA

Every security decision comes down to three properties. Understanding these isn't about memorizing definitions‚Äîit's about recognizing which one matters most in different situations.

Confidentiality: Keeping Secrets

Definition: Only authorized people can access the information.

üî¥ Attack Scenario: Someone gains access to your Namecheap account and sees your domain registrar credentials. Now they can see your DNS configuration, email forwarding rules, and payment methods.

Real Impact: Reconnaissance for future attacks. They know your infrastructure layout now.

Integrity: Trust the Truth

Definition: Data hasn't been tampered with. What you see is what was intended.

üî¥ Attack Scenario: An attacker compromises your GitHub account and modifies a Flipper Zero lesson. They inject a malicious BadUSB script that students copy and run, thinking it's from you. The script steals credentials.

Real Impact: Supply chain attack. Your students get compromised, your reputation is destroyed, and you lose all trust permanently. This is catastrophic‚Äîworse than losing confidentiality or availability.

Availability: Keeping the Lights On

Definition: Authorized users can access the system when they need it.

üî¥ Attack Scenario: DDoS attack floods your hosting provider. The academy goes offline for 48 hours.

Real Impact: Revenue loss, reputation damage, student frustration. Bad, but recoverable. You can tweet "we're under attack, back soon" and most people understand.

Key Insight: CIA is a framework, not a checklist. Different assets require different priorities. A payment processor needs Integrity above all (wrong amounts = disaster). A news site needs Availability (offline during breaking news = lost revenue). Your academy needs Integrity first (malicious lessons = game over).

Crown Jewels: What Actually Matters

Not all assets are created equal. Losing your Twitter password is annoying. Losing your domain registrar credentials is apocalyptic. Let's identify the crown jewels using tw¬°st3d_¬°nk Academy as the case study.

Threat Modeling tw¬°st3d_¬°nk Academy

Here are the critical assets, ranked by impact if compromised:

üî¥ Tier 1: Nuclear Keys (Domain & Infrastructure)

Assets: Namecheap registrar account, GitHub Pages hosting, DNS control

üü† Tier 2: Supply Chain (Content Integrity)

üü° Tier 3: Trust Foundation (User Data)

üîµ Tier 4: Revenue Stream (Availability)

Assets: Site uptime, GitHub Pages reliability

If compromised: Lost revenue, student frustration, but recoverable.

Defense priority: Medium. Backups, monitoring, incident response plan.

‚ö™ Tier 5: Soft Targets (Reputation)

Assets: Social media accounts, public presence

If compromised: Embarrassing, annoying, but not catastrophic.

The Key Question: "If I could only defend ONE thing perfectly, what would it be?" For tw¬°st3d_¬°nk Academy, the answer is Tier 1: Domain & Infrastructure. Because if that falls, you can't even tell people you've been compromised. You're locked out of your own operation.

Defense Priorities: Why Integrity > Confidentiality > Availability

Here's the controversial take that makes sense when you think like an attacker:

Integrity First: Supply Chain Attacks Are Permanent

If someone injects malicious code into your lessons and students get hacked, your reputation is destroyed forever. It doesn't matter if you catch it in 10 minutes‚Äîthe damage is done. Trust is the only non-renewable resource in education/security.

üî¥ Real World Example: In 2020, SolarWinds was compromised and malicious code was injected into their software updates. Even though SolarWinds caught it eventually, 18,000+ customers were already compromised. The brand never recovered.

Confidentiality Second: Reconnaissance Enables Future Attacks

If someone steals your domain credentials but doesn't use them immediately, they now have a persistent backdoor. They can study your infrastructure, plan the perfect attack, and strike when it hurts most.

Availability Last: Downtime Is Recoverable

If your site goes down, you can tweet "We're under attack, back soon." Most people understand. You lose revenue, but you don't lose trust. And trust is the only thing you can't buy back.

Exception: If you're running a hospital's patient monitoring system, Availability becomes Tier 1 because downtime = people die. Context matters. But for an educational platform? Integrity is king.

Single Points of Failure: The Email Revelation

Here's the insight that changes everything about how you architect security:

The Problem

Most people use ONE email address for everything:

  • Domain registrar (Namecheap)
  • Hosting (GitHub)
  • Social media
  • Public contact (listed on site)
  • Password resets

What happens when that email gets compromised?

üî¥ Attack Chain:
  1. Attacker compromises your email (phishing, data breach, weak password)
  2. Uses "forgot password" on Namecheap ‚Üí domain stolen
  3. Uses "forgot password" on GitHub ‚Üí repository hijacked
  4. Redirects domain to phishing site, injects malware into lessons
  5. Game over. Total compromise in <30 minutes.

The Solution: Email Segregation

Separate your Tier 1 email (domain/hosting control) from everything else:

  • Tier 1 Email: registrar@privatedomain.com (ONLY for Namecheap/DNS, never shared publicly)
  • Hosting Email: hosting@privatedomain.com (ONLY for GitHub/hosting)
  • Public Email: contact@twist3dink.xyz (listed on site, expect compromise)
  • Social Email: social@twist3dink.xyz (Twitter, Discord, etc.)

Why this matters: If your public email gets compromised, the attacker can't reset your domain password because they don't know the Tier 1 email even exists. You've broken the attack chain.

Defense in Depth: Even with email segregation, you still need hardware MFA on the Tier 1 email. But now you're defending from multiple angles. If MFA fails, email segregation catches it. If email segregation fails, MFA catches it. Layered defenses.
