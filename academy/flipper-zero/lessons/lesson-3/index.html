Lesson 3: Sub-GHz Fundamentals

Estimated time: 25 minutes | XP Reward: 50

What is Sub-GHz?

Sub-GHz refers to radio frequencies below 1 gigahertz (GHz). These frequencies are part of the electromagnetic spectrum used for wireless communication over short to medium distances. Unlike WiFi or Bluetooth which operate at 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz, sub-GHz frequencies offer better penetration through walls and obstacles, making them ideal for remote control applications.

Common Frequencies

Flipper Zero can transmit and receive on several sub-GHz bands, though available frequencies depend on your region due to regulatory restrictions:

  • 315 MHz: Popular in North America for garage door openers, car key fobs, and security systems
  • 433 MHz: Widely used internationally for remote controls, wireless doorbells, weather stations, and IoT devices
  • 868 MHz: Common in Europe for home automation, smart meters, and industrial sensors
  • 915 MHz: Used in North America for similar applications as 868 MHz in Europe

Real-World Applications

Sub-GHz technology is everywhere in our daily lives:

  • Garage Door Openers: Most residential garage doors use 310-390 MHz signals
  • Car Key Fobs: Remote unlock, lock, and trunk release functions
  • Wireless Doorbells: Battery-powered transmitters that don't require wiring
  • Weather Stations: Outdoor sensors transmitting temperature and humidity data
  • Tire Pressure Monitors: Sensors in vehicle tires reporting pressure levels
  • Home Security Systems: Window and door sensors communicating with alarm panels
Why Sub-GHz? Lower frequencies travel farther and penetrate obstacles better than higher frequencies. A 433 MHz signal can easily pass through walls, making it perfect for remote controls that need to work from outside your home or across large distances.

How Sub-GHz Communication Works

Understanding the basics of radio communication helps you make sense of what Flipper is capturing and replaying.

Transmitter and Receiver Basics

Every wireless system has two components:

  • Transmitter: Converts electrical signals into radio waves and broadcasts them through an antenna (your car key fob button press)
  • Receiver: Captures radio waves with an antenna and converts them back into electrical signals (the car's receiver module)

When you press a button on a remote control, the transmitter encodes your command (like "unlock door") into a specific radio signal pattern and broadcasts it. The receiver listens for this pattern and, when recognized, executes the corresponding action.

Modulation Types

Modulation is how information is encoded onto a radio carrier wave. Think of it as different "languages" for transmitting data:

  • AM (Amplitude Modulation): Changes the signal strength to encode data, similar to how AM radio works
  • FM (Frequency Modulation): Varies the frequency slightly to encode information
  • ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying): Simple on/off modulation, like Morse code with radio waves
  • FSK (Frequency Shift Keying): Switches between two frequencies to represent binary data (0s and 1s)

Flipper Zero automatically detects the modulation type used by most common devices, making signal analysis easier.

Signal Strength and Range

The effective range of sub-GHz communication depends on several factors:

  • Transmitter power output (typically 10-100 milliwatts for consumer devices)
  • Antenna design and quality
  • Environmental obstacles (walls, metal structures, interference)
  • Receiver sensitivity

Flipper Zero's range varies by frequency but typically matches or slightly exceeds the original device's range when replaying captured signals.

Using Flipper's Sub-GHz Module

Navigate to the Sub-GHz menu from the home screen to access five primary functions:

Read RAW

This is your go-to tool for capturing unknown or unrecognized signals. Raw mode records the actual radio waveform without trying to decode it, making it useful for:

  • Capturing signals from devices Flipper doesn't recognize
  • Recording complex or proprietary protocols
  • Analyzing timing and modulation patterns

Raw captures are larger files and may not work perfectly on replay, but they're invaluable for learning and experimentation.

Read

Use this mode when you want Flipper to automatically identify and decode known protocols. When activated, Flipper listens on the configured frequency and displays decoded information when it recognizes a signal. This mode works with:

  • Princeton protocol (common in garage openers and simple remotes)
  • KeeLoq (used in many car key fobs)
  • Star Line (vehicle security systems)
  • And dozens of other recognized protocols

Saved

Access your library of previously captured signals. Each saved file includes the frequency, modulation, and protocol information needed to replay it. You can organize saves into folders for easy management.

Add Manually

For advanced users, this allows creating custom signals by manually entering frequency, modulation type, and data values. Useful for reconstructing known protocols or testing specific signal parameters.

Frequency Analyzer

This powerful tool scans the radio spectrum to find active frequencies in your environment. It displays signal strength across different frequencies, helping you identify which channels are in use. Perfect for discovering what frequency a device uses before attempting to capture it.

Pro Tip: Always start with Frequency Analyzer when working with an unknown device. It tells you exactly where to look, saving time and increasing your chances of a successful capture.

Capturing a Signal

Let's walk through capturing a signal from a wireless device you own:

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Identify your target: Choose a device you own (wireless doorbell, garage remote, etc.)
  2. Open Sub-GHz menu: Navigate to Sub-GHz from the main menu
  3. Select capture mode:
    • Use "Read" if you know the device uses a common protocol
    • Use "Read RAW" for unknown devices or better compatibility
  4. Set frequency: Select the appropriate frequency for your region and device (often 433.92 MHz or 315 MHz)
  5. Position devices: Hold your remote near Flipper (within 1 foot for best results)
  6. Start capture: Press OK to begin listening
  7. Activate remote: Press the button on your device once
  8. Observe feedback: Flipper will show signal detection and strength
  9. Save capture: Press OK to save, then name your file descriptively

Reading the Signal Data

After capturing, Flipper displays information about the signal:

  • Frequency: The exact frequency detected (e.g., 433.920 MHz)
  • Protocol: Identified protocol name (if recognized)
  • Key: The unique data code transmitted
  • Bit length: Number of bits in the transmission
  • TE (Time Element): Timing information for the signal

Saving Captures

When saving, you'll be prompted to name your file. Use descriptive names that help you remember what the capture does:

  • ✓ Good: "garage_door_open" or "doorbell_frontdoor"
  • ✗ Poor: "signal1" or "test"

Naming Conventions

Develop a consistent naming system to stay organized:

  • Include device type: garage, car, doorbell, fan
  • Include function: open, close, unlock, button1
  • Include location if relevant: frontdoor, bedroom, workshop
  • Use underscores or hyphens instead of spaces
Troubleshooting Captures: If Flipper doesn't detect your signal, try moving closer to the device, checking that you're on the correct frequency, or using Read RAW mode for better compatibility.

Replaying Signals

Once you've captured a signal, replaying it is straightforward - but there are important considerations.

How to Transmit Saved Signals

  1. Navigate to Sub-GHz → Saved
  2. Browse to your saved signal file
  3. Select the file and press OK
  4. Choose "Emulate" or "Send" option
  5. Press OK to transmit the signal

Flipper will broadcast the saved signal at the same frequency and with the same modulation as captured. The transmission typically lasts a fraction of a second, just like pressing the original remote button.

Legal Considerations

⚠️ CRITICAL LEGAL WARNING

Only replay signals for devices you own or have explicit permission to control. Transmitting signals to systems you don't own is illegal and can result in:

  • Criminal charges for unauthorized access
  • FCC violations (United States) or equivalent regulatory penalties
  • Civil liability for damages
  • Confiscation of equipment

Testing on Your Own Devices Only

The golden rule: If you don't own it, don't transmit to it. This includes:

  • Your neighbor's garage door
  • Vehicles in a parking lot
  • Store security systems
  • Smart home devices in other people's homes

Testing on your own devices is legal and educational. It helps you understand security vulnerabilities in your own systems and make informed decisions about upgrades or security improvements.

Common Sub-GHz Protocols

Understanding protocol types helps you predict what you can and cannot capture or replay.

Rolling Codes vs Fixed Codes

Fixed Code Systems:

  • Transmit the same signal every time you press the button
  • Simple and cheap to implement
  • Can be captured and replayed perfectly
  • Common in older garage doors, basic remotes, and budget devices
  • Security vulnerability: anyone who captures the signal can replay it

Rolling Code Systems (Hopping Codes):

  • Generate a new, unique code for each button press
  • Both transmitter and receiver stay synchronized using a shared algorithm
  • After use, each code becomes invalid
  • Common in modern car key fobs and newer garage openers
  • Much more secure against replay attacks

Princeton Protocol

One of the most common fixed-code protocols. Used in countless inexpensive wireless devices including doorbells, simple RF remotes, and older garage openers. Princeton signals are easy to capture and replay.

KeeLoq Protocol

A rolling code system used primarily in automotive key fobs and some garage door openers. KeeLoq is designed to prevent replay attacks by changing the code with each transmission. While Flipper can capture KeeLoq signals, replaying them typically won't work due to the rolling code mechanism.

Why Some Signals Can't Be Replayed

Several factors can prevent successful signal replay:

  • Rolling codes: The captured code has already been used and is now invalid
  • Encryption: Signal data is encrypted and can't be decoded without keys
  • Challenge-response: System requires bidirectional communication
  • Time-based codes: Signal includes timestamp validation
  • Regional restrictions: Flipper may block certain frequencies in your region
Learning Opportunity: When you encounter a signal that can't be replayed, it's an opportunity to learn about why. Research the protocol and understand the security mechanisms that protect it. This knowledge makes you a better security professional.

Legal & Safety Warnings

⚠️ REGULATORY COMPLIANCE REQUIRED

Radio frequency transmissions are regulated by government agencies worldwide. Understanding and following these regulations is not optional.

FCC Regulations (United States)

The Federal Communications Commission regulates all radio transmissions in the US:

  • Part 15 Compliance: Flipper operates under Part 15 rules as an unlicensed transmitter
  • Power Limits: Must not exceed specified power output limits for each frequency band
  • Interference: Must not cause harmful interference to licensed services
  • Frequency Restrictions: Some frequencies are restricted or require licenses

Jamming is Illegal

Intentionally interfering with wireless communications is a serious federal crime:

  • Jamming or blocking any radio frequency is illegal
  • This includes WiFi, cellular, GPS, emergency services, and sub-GHz bands
  • Penalties include fines up to $112,500 and imprisonment
  • Flipper Zero is not designed for jamming and attempting to use it this way violates federal law

Only Test Your Own Devices

We cannot overstate this enough: transmitting signals to control or interact with devices you don't own is illegal. Period. This applies even if:

  • You're "just testing" or "curious"
  • The device is in a public place
  • You think the owner won't notice
  • You plan to tell the owner afterward

Legal security testing requires written authorization and proper contracts. Unauthorized testing is hacking, regardless of intent.

Interference Concerns

Even when testing your own devices, be mindful of unintended interference:

  • Your transmission might accidentally affect nearby similar devices
  • Multiple transmissions in quick succession could disrupt neighbors' equipment
  • Test in controlled environments when possible
  • If you notice your actions affecting others' systems, stop immediately
Ethical Standard: True professionals in security and technology prioritize responsible disclosure and ethical behavior. The skills you're learning should make technology more secure, not exploit it for personal gain or amusement.

Key Takeaway

Sub-GHz radio communication is one of Flipper Zero's most powerful and educational features. Understanding how wireless protocols work gives you insight into the invisible radio waves constantly surrounding us, from car key fobs to wireless doorbells to home security systems.

The ability to capture and analyze these signals is incredibly valuable for security research, device testing, and understanding technology. However, this power must be wielded responsibly. Always:

  • Test only devices you own
  • Respect others' privacy and property
  • Follow local radio frequency regulations
  • Use knowledge to improve security, not exploit vulnerabilities

As you experiment with Sub-GHz, you'll develop an intuition for how these systems work, which protocols are secure, and where vulnerabilities exist. This knowledge is the foundation for becoming a skilled security researcher.
